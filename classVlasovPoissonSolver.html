Detailed Description

template<class Mapping>
class VlasovPoissonSolver< Mapping >

Solve the Poisson equation and return the electric field for the coupled Vlasov equation.

The Vlasov-Poisson equations are given by

  • (1) \( \partial_t \rho - E_y \partial_x \rho + E_x \partial_y\rho = 0 \),
  • (2) \( - \Delta \phi = \rho \),
  • (3) and \( E = -\nabla \phi \).

The functions are defined on a logical domain, and the mapping from the logical domain to the physical domain is written \(\mathcal{F}\).

We here focus on equations (2) and (3). We first compute \( \phi \) on B-splines with the given Poisson solver. Then in the VlasovPoissonSolver::operator() we compute the electric field thanks to (3) using the B-splines coefficients. Depending on the given mapping, the computation at the center point is not always well-defined so we linearize around the center point as explained in Edoardo Zoni's article (https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jcp.2019.108889).

With (3), we compute the electric field

  • \( E(r, \theta) = -\nabla_{x,y} \phi(r, \theta) \),

with \(\nabla_{x,y} \phi(r, \theta) \) from \( \nabla_{x,y} \phi(r, \theta) = (J_{\mathcal{F}}^{-1})^T \nabla_{r, \theta} \phi(r, \theta) \),

and \( \nabla \phi = \sum_{ij} \partial_{x_i} \phi g^{i,j} e_j \) with \( g^{i,j} \) the coefficients of the inverse metric tensor and \( e_j \) the unnormalized local covariant base:

  • \( \nabla \phi(r,\theta) \cdot e_r = \partial_r \phi(r,\theta) g^{1,1} + \partial_\theta \phi(r,\theta)g^{2,1} \),
  • \( \nabla \phi(r,\theta) \cdot e_\theta = \partial_r \phi(r,\theta) g^{1,2} + \partial_\theta \phi(r,\theta)g^{2,2} \).

For \( r < \varepsilon \), \( E(r, \theta) = \left( 1 - \frac{r}{\varepsilon} \right) E(0, \theta) + \frac{r}{\varepsilon} E(\varepsilon, \theta) \),

with \( E(0, \theta) \) computed thanks to

  • \( \partial_r \phi (0, \theta_1) = \left[\partial_r x \partial_x \phi + \partial_r y \partial_y \phi \right](0, \theta_1) \),
  • \( \partial_r \phi (0, \theta_2) = \left[\partial_r x \partial_x \phi + \partial_r y \partial_y \phi \right] (0, \theta_2) \),

where \( \theta_1 \) and \( \theta_2 \) correspond to linearly independent directions.

The equation (1) is computed thanks to advection operator.
